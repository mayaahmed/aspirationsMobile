Chapter 19

Difficult times

Soon there would be personal trouble. Arti's father had to go for a minor surgery but it resulted in temporary memory loss, incontinence, and difficulty in movement. He was diagnosed with Normal Pressure Hydrocephalus which meant that the brain held on to excess water. The doctors recommended brain surgery. Arti's family was hesitant about another surgery because one surgery had lead to this condition for her father. Arti rushed back home to see her father. It was heart wrenching to see her father so changed. He could hardly speak or remember what he did a few minutes before. He could not walk and most of the time he spend sitting on a chair or lying in bed.

Arti decided that before they go in for surgery they would try everything else possible. They got Ayurvedic doctors to massage Arti's father and he showed remarkable improvement. So Arti came back to the US thinking that in time her father would recover. But his condition got worse.

Something had gone wrong in Arti's professional life too. Arti found herself without a job after a PhD in mathematics. She tried to make sense of the situation.

"These are difficult times" said one professor. "Jobs are difficult to come by."

This argument did not make sense. A lot of people who were graduating with her had job offers and more than one for that matter. She had been a consistently better student than all of them. It did not make sense that her department thought it was normal for her to not find employment.

"It is alright" said a colleague. "That blond guy with blue eyes did not get a job either."

"Sorry. I cannot feel better that I do not have a job just because a blond guy with blue eyes did not find one" said Arti irritated.

"The scientific committee decided that Americans should find jobs before immigrants" said another colleague.

"How do you know?" asked Arti. "None of the job advertisements say that. It is preposterous to assume that most mathematicians anywhere in the world can do the kind of work I do."

"We love immigrants" he continued. "This country was build by immigrants. But we must take care of Americans who were here first."

"Whatever!" said Arti.

Arti was facing a lot of new arguments. Arti had never faced discrimination in her life in America before. The teachers were always more than fair. In an American classroom it never had mattered who she was or where she came from. It only mattered that she was interested and ready to work hard. This attitude was new to her. An ominous change was sweeping across America and this was only the beginning.

But what made Arti really angry is when she heard people close to her say

"There are some dreams that do not come true."

"No one would work hard towards a dream if they had this attitude" Arti would reply angrily every time to that statement.

Arti was in no mood to reassure people that it was fine to be unemployed. This habit of people annoyed her. People visit hospitals and are greatly reassured when the sick man tells them he is fine. He is not fine till he recovers. There was nothing fine about being unemployed either. In time, if things got better she would stand out as an example of how in spite of things going horribly wrong she survived. It annoyed her that she was to be that kind of example.

Arti was happy she was married and she had a home to come back to when things had gone wrong. She worried about other scientists who did not have this choice.

"It is not enough if an individual strives to succeed" said Kavita. "We came so far because the entire system was geared to bring us here. All efforts of an individual are wasted in a faulty system."

"A pitcher full of holes cannot retain water" said Kabir.

There was a silver lining in these dark clouds and that was that Arti had free time. Her father was ill and she could attend to him. Her siblings had jobs and were very busy. Thus Arti traveled to India to take care of her father. His disease had progressed and his condition had worsened. Brain surgery seemed to be the last resort. Any doubts that Arti had about heading for surgery subsided when she met the surgeon. He looked like he knew his job.

The night before the surgery Arti's relatives came from far and wide. The hospital room was filled with visitors. Late in to the night people recalled stories from their memory. They laughed about events in the past. Arti was afraid that laughing too much might bring bad luck. She was really frightened about her father's surgery. But it was good to have family around. They reduced stress considerably. All through the hospital stay visitors took the focus away from the sick environment of the hospital room. India truly was a cradle of affection.

After the surgery Arti's father was physically very weak. He seemed to be slightly more mentally aware. There was no visible progress in his condition. It was depressing and Arti resigned herself to never having a real conversation with her father again. Arti left India with a heavy heart. Her father was a mess and may never recover again.
