Chapter 14

The Farm

Arti's parents did not live in Mumbai anymore. It was typical for many elder people to move out from Mumbai and retire in smaller towns or villages. Arti's parents moved to a village in the south of India. Arti had not seen the place yet and she was really thrilled when she finally got time to visit them.

"I am glad we are going to my father's farmhouse. I wonder what it is like" said Arti.

Arti and Kabir traveled by bus to the farmhouse. It was an eight hour journey and people preferred to travel at night. That way they planned to sleep through the journey.

The bus was a pretty fancy bus compared to the local buses. Arti heard a woman complaint that the bus did not have a video player. But it did have a cassette player and all through the night they played songs real loud. Once in a while there was silence and someone in the bus would scold the driver for not starting the music soon.

"What a way to travel! These people do like to suffer and make others suffer with their ridiculous music" complained Kabir.

Arti was glad when it was morning.

"At least there is some good scenery outside. If you cannot sleep at least you can enjoy the view" said Arti.

The bus had traveled quite far from Bangalore and in that morning light Arti felt she was in paradise. The red mud, the tall tropical trees, and the sounds of the birds along the long winding roads was a heady mix.

"I wonder what my father's house is like" said Arti. "I hope it is like my grandfather's house in Kerala. My grandfather's house was at the foothills of a hill. The backyard opened into a large black rock. The house was surrounded by rows and rows of trees. I hope my father's house is like that."

"You will be seeing it soon. So you can stop wondering" said Kabir.

"The black rock where I sat many a lazy summer afternoons thinking and wondering about my future. Big black rocks can make you philosophical" said Arti musing.

She could see herself as a little girl roaming on the rock.

"The rows and rows of rubber trees and a road far away. I always had a feeling that someone special would come from that road. It was like that road so far away would bring adventure. I wonder what my father's house is like."

Arti's father was at the bus stop to pick her up.

"Papa, I wonder what our house is like. Is it like that house there?" asked Arti pointing to a house.

"No. It is better" her father said.

All along the way Arti pointed to houses asking her father whether their house was like that and her father kept saying that their house was better.

Finally they reached their farmhouse. At first Arti was disappointed. Not because the house was not good, but it was different from what she thought it would be. There was no black rock and for some reason she was so sure there would be a black rock behind her house.

"Where is the stream?" asked Arti.

Arti could not think of a farm house without a stream. Her mother had told her that their farmhouse did have a stream.

"I will show you" said her mother and she took her behind the house and showed her the stream. It was a little stream and there were small fish in it.

"Oh! I wanted a big stream" said Arti.

"This is all that we have" said Arti's mother a little upset that Arti was not thrilled with the little stream.

"Sorry, Ma. I am adjusting my mental image to reality. It is quite a nice place" said Arti.

Soon her father's farm house defined itself in Arti's mind. It was different from her grandfather's house. It was unique and interesting in its own way. The neighbors stopped by for the whole village knew that Arti was visiting.

"Arti, come and say hello to this chedati (aunt)" her mother called out.

When Arti went to the front porch, there was a little crowd of old women gathered there to meet her. These were her mother's friends. Arti greeted them. The women were staring at Arti and one woman asked her mother where the son in law was. Arti's mother pointed him out to them. Kabir waved to them from where he was and the ladies giggled. Arti was at a loss of words. She stared at them and they at her.

"Can you speak Malayalam?" one lady asked.

"Of course I can speak Malayalam" said Arti. "Where do you live?"

Arti congratulated herself for having found this question. The question was politically correct, respectful and also proved that she could talk Malayalam. There was no way Arti could get into trouble asking people where they lived. Her mother seemed to approve of that question and looked on beaming with pride. Arti asked every new person she met over the next few days the same question.

"Conversation skills require some standard questions" declared Arti.

Soon Arti had settled herself in the farmhouse.

"Walking on your land, the land you own is a great feeling" said Arti. "In Mumbai, our flats are so small. Here even the poorest of the poor have a little land of their own. Mumbai steals something fundamental from all of us by being so crowded."

The little farm was full of surprises. The rows and rows of banana, coconut, and cashew trees belonged to her father. Kabir spend all the time breaking coconuts from the trees and drinking the water. Arti's mother owned many chickens. She showed Arti the collection of eggs she would gather each morning into a little basket. Arti would steal eggs and give it to the farm dog. The farm dog would hold the egg very carefully and punch a nail into the egg shell and lick the inside of the egg slowly.

"Don't waste all my good eggs" her mother would scold.

"What is life but a few interesting moments. If we look back in time, life is not thirty years but some thirty events. The days I spend in the farm are among those events" said Arti on her way back to Bangalore.
