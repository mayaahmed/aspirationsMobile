Chapter 5

A Malayali Wedding

The exhibition ended and like many other things that year, the ending of a school exhibition was a sad event. It meant she had to move on and the stress of the nearing Board exam was the only thing that prevented Kavita from becoming gloomy. She was busy. Anyone who met her these days would first ask her how her preparations for the board exam was going. Admissions to colleges were competitive and tough. Kavita tried her best to look very concerned. But on many occasions when she was alone she found her books tedious to read and she could not care less how she did her exams.

"Look at the pictures of the students that came in the merit list last year. I cut these pictures for you. If your name comes in the merit list, I will be so proud" her mother said.

"High hopes, ma. I hope I pass" said Kavita.

"You could come in the merit list. You only have to work hard" her mother said.

"No way I can work that hard" said Kavita firmly.

"You could, if you tried" her mother said really disappointed. Looking at her mother's sad face, Kavita almost had an urge to try to make it to the merit list. But one look at the books in front her made her soon forget that the thought ever occurred to her.

"Three more months to go!" sighed Kavita.

The sun was setting and evenings often made Kavita pensive. Another day was up and she felt she had not got much done. She could have worked more. The board exams were near.

"Switch on the lights, Kavita. It will get dark soon. One should turn on the lights before other people always" said her mother.

"Why?" asked Kavita.

"I don't know. People say like that" her mother said.

"If everyone tried to switch lights on before everyone else then we would never be able to switch lights off" said Kavita.

"One should be reasonable about one's superstitions. You don't have to get carried away with the idea" scolded Kavita's mother.

"How come you believe in all these superstitions without figuring out what they signify?" asked Kavita.

"Don't argue. If you do not want to switch the lights on, just say so. I will do it myself" said her mother.

"I will do it, mother. Just wondering. That is all" said Kavita.

Kavita turned on the lights of the first room. That was the room that should be lit. The rule did not apply to other rooms of the house. Kavita went out and stood in the balcony. There was a cold wind blowing. She could see people hurrying back home. Mostly men returning from work with packages in their hands. The number of children playing in the playgrounds was constantly dwindling. There was some logic in switching on the lights before the sun set. Some of the houses that were not lit yet appeared gloomy. The lights made everything alive. Kavita watched the birds settling down in large numbers on the trees for the night. Birds of so many different kinds rested in harmony on the same tree for the night. Why did this harmony exist? Nature was strange in its ways. Where one expected conflict there was harmony, and where one expected harmony there was conflict.

"I am going to Pooja's house. I need a walk" said Kavita.

"Come back soon. It will get really dark soon" said Kavita's mother.

Kavita and Pooja walked around the building a few times before deciding to settle on a cement seat in front of the building. The cement seat was popular and was rarely vacant.

"So many people are outside their homes at this time of the day. Why?" asked Kavita.

"I think people are taking leave of each other. As they retire to their homes for the night they are taking leave of their neighbors. Or maybe they are exchanging notes about the day's proceedings. Who knows? We should one day do a survey on what these women are discussing in the evenings" said Pooja.

"One has to pause a little in the evenings. The electric lights replace the sunlight and soon it makes no difference in the modern world whether it is day or night. But there is a little intermission and one reluctantly notices the change. A day is spend and life goes on" said Kavita.

"Boy! What is wrong with you?" asked Pooja.

"It is all this studying. I want to avoid thinking about my school work and my mind goes on all these tangents. It happens more when exams are nearing" said Kavita.

"What about Amar? Is he a pleasant distraction still?" asked Pooja laughing.

"Not after he made all those faces" said Kavita laughing. "Those faces were unwarranted. Did he seriously expect people to know what the Pons Varoli of the brain was?"

"Never laugh at a man when he is trying to appear smart. It is no trivial thing to know what pons varoli of the brain is" said Pooja.

"It reminds me of a poem someone wrote which says that when a man thinks of you with anger and his words have turned bitter then it is time to let him go" said Kavita.

"I wonder where making faces fits in this framework" said Pooja.

"Who knows? I know of no poems on a man making faces. This sure is a new phenomenon" said Kavita.

"It sure is" said Pooja.

"I know of no poems that talk about Pons varoli either" said Kavita.

"Now what exactly is Pons varoli? What is its function?" asked Pooja.

"Beats me. That piques my interest. So I am going back to hit my biology books" said Kavita.

"Me too" said Pooja and the two girls went back home to study.

When Kavita got home her mother was busy reading letters. Kavita's mother had advertised in the newspaper for a husband for her sister. Every day ten or twenty letters would come by mail and once in a while her mother sorted out the letters she would reply to.

"I selected these ten letters to reply to" said Kavita's mother.

Kavita browsed the letters her mother had selected.

"This man wants to know the details of dowry? Isn't dowry illegal? We should notify the police about this man" said Kavita.

"I do not think asking for dowry is illegal. Harassing someone for dowry is illegal" clarified Kavita's mother.

"I can check with the police" said Kavita offering her help.

"Keep out of this Kavita. Why are you talking about the police when someone is arranging a marriage? Talk auspiciously" scolded Kavita's mother.

"Alright" said Kavita.

She looked through the letters her mother had rejected.

"Why are these rejected?" asked Kavita.

"They are not Malayali Christians like us" said her mother.

"This man is a Malayali Christian" said Kavita really happy to be of help for a change.

"We belong to a different sect. They are protestants" explained her mother.

Kavita's mother then went on to explain how they were Brahmins who were converted to Christianity by Saint Thomas himself.

"What if all that is a lie? There is no proof" said Kavita.

"There is plenty of proof that Saint Thomas came to Kerala. It is in the bible" said her mother.

"May be" said Kavita. "But there is no proof he converted our forefathers. Also there is no proof that he converted Brahmins. Anyways, mother, Christ himself preferred fishermen. Fishermen are not among the higher castes in Kerala. On second thoughts I am convinced that our forefathers were fishermen."

"The very idea! We are the Syro Malabar people. We are converted by Saint Thomas himself" said her mother angrily.

Kavita decided to let go of the argument. She watched her mother write letters describing the virtues of her aunt and the details of the dowry. This was Mumbai and there was no way one could check the caste of a person. One Thomas was as good as any. They came from Kerala to try their fortune in a new land called Mumbai. The Malayali people were a hard working lot and were a preferred work force in Mumbai offices. The matrimonial advertisements in the daily newspaper Times of India was very much an acquired culture of the Mumbai Malayalis. But it worked very well. Within a few weeks Kavita's aunt was getting married.

Kavita's mother and aunt went with the boy's family to pick up two saris. A white sari her aunt would wear for the wedding and a cream silk sari with red border that she would wear for the reception.

"Only two saris?" asked Nutan who was Kavita's neighbor.

"Yes. It is not like Hindu weddings. Christian weddings are very simple" explained Kavita.

"I am sure it is. Even the reception sari has such mild color compared to the vibrant yellows and reds of a Hindu wedding" said Nutan.

"So it is" said Kavita. She was not sure whether she was happy about that fact. Why was Christian weddings so simple? But the Kerala Hindu weddings were quite simple too. It was shorter than Kerala christian weddings.

"Why are the people of Kerala so simple? Why are we not grand like others?" Kavita asked her mother.

"That is our culture. We are simple people" her mother said.

"But why is our culture like that? Why don't we apply turmeric and sandalwood paste before a wedding? Why don't we put bright red kumkums on our forehead?" asked Kavita.

"I have work to do" said her mother and quickly disappeared.

But on the wedding day, Kavita's aunt looked pretty in a white sari, with a little white crown on her head, and a white bouquet of flowers in her hand.

"I wish we had little crowns to wear in our weddings" said Nutan.

"That is life. Everyone wishes for what they do not have. Man is never satisfied" said Kavita.

Kavita's aunt did not look bad in the cream sari either. Plenty of gold ornaments made up for the simplicity of the sari. When the wedding was over her aunt left with her newly wedded husband in a car decorated with rose flowers.

"I guess every culture has its charm. Also every culture knows what works best for it" said Kavita.

Kavita wondered what was the image of herself as a bride. A red sari with a bindi on her forehead and jasmines in the hair like a Hindu bride or would she be in a white sari with a bouquet in her hand? Why did she assume she had a choice? Kavita realized that she had never bracketed herself as a Malayali girl destined to marry a Malayali man. She was free and she cherished the thought. And to her surprise, she also realized that she did not like the image of herself as a bride at all. Marriage seemed unpalatable to her. It was okay to see others dressed up like a doll and walking in to slavery. That was not a fate she wished for herself. And somehow after that thought the books in front of her looked more inviting than ever. Education was the key to true freedom. Tedious though the path maybe, freedom of spirit was worth the trouble.
