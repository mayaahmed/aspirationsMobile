Chapter 18

About being a Mathematician

Arti met Rajini at a grocery store. As soon as Rajini heard Arti was a mathematician Rajini wanted Arti to inspire her children to do math.

"Arti, since you are working on a PhD in mathematics, perhaps you can motivate my children to do mathematics" said Rajini.

"I am not good at inspiring little children to study math" said Arti.

"I want them to meet you" said Rajini enthusiastically. "I want you to talk to them."

"I will try to tell them math is fun" said Arti with a shrug.

A lunch was planned and Arti set out to motivate Rajini's children to study mathematics. One look at the children and Arti gave up on her plans. It was ridiculous to tell children hardly five years of age to study mathematics. Arti instead spend time playing with them.

Later Arti told Rajini

"Children learn by imitating their parents or their peers. You cannot lecture them in to getting interested in anything. A one year child is very interested in the pots and pans he sees his mother use more than any other toy you might give him to play with. The best way to teach children math is by finding a reason to do math yourself."

"At this age, you surely do not expect me to study mathematics" said Rajini laughing.

"Then you cannot expect them to study mathematics at their age either" said Arti conclusively.

"But they have to" said Rajini. "Times have changed. The world is a lot more competitive now."

Why every child should find math fun because the world became competitive was not clear. There would always be jobs that did not require much math skills. A lot of well paying jobs and careers did not require math skills at all. But Arti did not want to agitate Rajini. Rajini knew what was best for her children. It was clear that most parents wanted their children to excel in mathematics.

"I will come back in a few years to inspire your children" said Arti as she took her leave. "I find them too young right now."

Arti had planned to study all her life. The idea that one had to be in a certain age group to study would not work in academics. Though being a mathematician was not as grand as being a film star, it did generate a certain kind of awe among people.

"You must be smart!" people would say to Arti time and again.

Arti did not particularly think of herself as smart. The only reason she became a mathematician was because mathematics was the easiest thing for her to do. Only a few people spend lot of time learning classical music. But the ones who did were not called smart. But people in general thought mathematicians were smart.

But there were a few who were not mathematicians but thought they knew more mathematics than mathematicians. Such people called upon Arti to solve all kinds of tricky problems. If she did not know the answer to some question the person asking questions would conclude that he was better than her at math. The problem was that like art almost every one was exposed to some level of mathematics. But mathematics was hard work. After hours of working on a problem, Arti would get physically tired. It was difficult to explain because brain work is not considered physically tiring. Moreover no one could question Arti's passion for mathematics. She would ignore every other activity to spend her time with books. Hard work and passion were the keys to becoming a professional at any skill. To be considered a mathematician it was not enough to have a few tricky questions up your sleeve. It was necessary to spend years doing mathematics.
