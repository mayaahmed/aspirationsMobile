Chapter 6

Remnants of Culture

Kavita had to stop at the Kerala Samaj to buy some arishtrams (tonics) and koyumbus (balms) for her mother.

"I like going to the Kerala Samaj. It smells so nice in there" said Pooja.

Kavita stopped at the little library in the entrance of the building to return some Malayalam books for her mother. The library smelled of old books and it was a pleasant smell. There were rows and rows of Malayalam books bound in a similar way. Kavita walked around looking at a few books, though she could not read Malayalam. The library was fascinating because apart from this tiny room, Kavita had not seen Malayalam books anywhere else. She felt fortunate that she knew of the existence of this place otherwise she would not know about the existence of these books. Malayali authors were churning out books by the dozens but none of the book stores of Mumbai were selling any copies. Only English books were visible in the market and maybe a few stray Hindi books. Many newspaper stalls sold Malayalam magazines but no books.

"It makes my soul happy that people of the state of Kerala have authors that write books. Someday I will read what they have to say. What do Indian thinkers have to tell us? What are they writing about? I wish I could read Malayalam well" said Kavita.

"The school books are bad enough for me. What with learning Hindi and Marathi I am up to my neck with languages. Please do not wish for Malayalam in the school curriculum. If you do, I will have to wish to undo your wish" said Pooja a little alarmed.

"We learn Hindi and Marathi only for namesake. Can we really read Hindi literature or Marathi literature? No. I wish I could at least read Malayalam literature. Malayalam is my mother tongue. I bet there are a lot of interesting ideas hidden in these volumes of books" said Kavita.

"It is fascinating because we do not understand. These alphabets, loud and clear, are declaring something that we do not comprehend. This is what is on the minds of our parents perhaps. You realize they are rather incomprehensible too. By reading Malayalam literature we can understand what they are thinking" said Pooja.

"Hmmm, that may be the reason why they are giving us an English education" said Kavita laughing.

After the library visit they went to the Ayurveda store. There was a little line before the prescription counter. People were talking to each other and introducing each other. Everyone seemed happy to be there. In all of Mumbai, the Kerala Samaj building stood alone in the service it provided the Malayalis. For Kavita, it was a place where she tried to understand her roots, her parents, and her ancestors.

The Ayurvedic center held medicines of all kinds. Some of the medicines were very bitter, some were very fragrant, and some were purely delicious. Kavita's mother had a habit of buying up different kinds of medicines and made Kavita and her siblings drink them. Her mother believed that Ayurveda, even if it did not do any good at least did not do any harm. She considered it as a part of good parenting to make children drink medicines whether they were ill or not. As long as the medicines did not taste awful, Kavita did not mind taking them. But she never agreed to drink bitter medicines. Kavita particularly liked the koyumbu she was buying that day. The koyumbu was for lessening aches and pain and it was made with pure ghee, sandalwood, and other fragrant ingredients. Kavita applied some koyumbu on her hands every time her parents used it because she liked the smell.

The Samaj organized dance and music classes. Before heading home Kavita and Pooja watched dance rehearsals for sometime. On the way out of the building the girls met Vellu who told them about the play that was going to be enacted in Shanmukananda Hall.

"Remind your parents to buy tickets for the show. I know they always buy tickets, but remind them anyways. This is a really nice play and before the play we will have our very own Kuruppu singing songs. Please remind your parents" he said.

"Sure we will" said Kavita.

When Vellu left Pooja said

"This place is filled with culture maniacs. I am almost sorry that I am not participating in any of these events."

"Yes. It plays on one's guilt. Forget these events which is just traces of a culture. I am guilty of not participating in the very essence of Malayali culture. That is, I do not consider myself a Malayali in the true sense. I feel all these things are shows people are putting up and I am merely a spectator. I am merely a spectator of the Malayali culture" said Kavita.

"I am like that too" said Pooja.

"In the process of human immigration, there is a constant rejection of culture. We redefine culture as we understand it. We adopt a potpourri of the many cultures we are exposed to in Mumbai. This culture has a character of its own. But at the same time we are rejecting the beliefs of our ancestors in one generation making our choices hard and confusing. In some sense I feel culturally deprived and I feel like a gypsy" said Kavita.

"The exams are really near" said Pooja a little worried.

"Yes. Exams that will decide our lives, they say" said Kavita.

"I hope we get into the science stream. I hope we get enough percentage of marks to get admissions to a good college" said Pooja.

"One strives as much as one can. I cannot push myself beyond what my character has intended me to do. If science is for me, then I will make the science stream effortlessly and elegantly. If not, then of course we know that the universe still extends beyond the stars. There is something else out there for me. What is there for me in the immediate future is the million dollar question. And unfortunately astrology is not good enough to answer that question" said Kavita.

"The future is what it maybe, but the present is to stare at a pile of books" said Pooja.

"So it is. The present is rather boringly clear" said Kavita.

Pooja took out a laddoo from her bag and offered it to Kavita.

"Here, eat a laddoo. We got it from our trip to the Tirupathi temple. They are the best laddoos in the world."

"I cannot" said Kavita. "It is the Lent season. I give up sweets for forty days. I agree these laddoos sure are the best laddoos in the world."

"Really? You cannot eat laddoos in Lent?" asked Pooja.

"It is a season of abstinence. I usually give up eating sweets. Some people give up smoking, others give up eating meat. It is a personal choice. But the idea is that one must sacrifice something" said Kavita.

"And you just gave up Tirupathi laddoos. It is abstinence indeed" said Pooja laughing.

"Also I go to church every day this week. This week is good Friday week. There is mass and novenas everyday" said Kavita.

"Are you going to the Matunga church?" asked Pooja.

"No, to our colony's church" said Kavita. "The Malayali church. The prayers are all in Malayalam. The whole mass is musical. I do not know the songs. But they sound very nice and touching."

"Your talk makes me want to visit temples. I don't go that often to temples" said Pooja philosophically.

Kavita was a little late when she reached the church. She found a vacant seat in the front benches and went and sat there. She looked around and found her mother on her knees, praying. Her mother nodded acknowledging her presence.

Kavita started walking along with the little crowd in the church as they stopped in front of different portraits of Christ which pictured the fourteen stations in the way of the cross. The songs they sang in church that day were all sad songs. The sufferings described in the songs were more general than the sufferings of Christ. Kavita's heart was heavy at the end of the mass and she fought back tears that were appearing in her eyes time and again.

Then it was time for the novena. And they started to sing "Nitya sahaya mathe" which was a prayer to Mary asking her to pray for her children. For Kavita, this song was like a call from her ancient past. This was among the few Malayalam songs she had heard before. On her trips to Kerala, she had heard this song every night. Her rural family gathered together every evening after dinner and in the light of kerosene lamps (for there was no electricity in the villages) prayed for almost an hour. In Mumbai homes the evening prayers were a rare occasion, but almost all the families Kavita visited in Kerala prayed in the evenings. And they always ended the prayers with the song Nitya sahaya mathe.

The song struck a chord in Kavita's soul. It was touching to see her grand parents, her parents, her aunts, uncles, cousins, and siblings on their knees praying to some mother in heaven asking to pray for them. It made human kind look so vulnerable. Somehow the faces of adults appeared very worried and helpless as they sang this song. And it was only during the Good Friday week that she heard this song in Mumbai. It was a very similar scene with so many adults on their knees looking very vulnerable as they asked Mary to pray for the thousands of hurting souls.

Kavita turned around to look for her mother again. Her mother was on her knees in a white sari, the pallu covering her head, her eyes closed, singing along with the crowd. Her mother was not a good singer and the thought made Kavita smile. But the sight of her mother reassured her. Her mother, proud, wise, and strong was on her knees and praying but that would pass. She would be strong and assuring again. It did not matter whether the world had a mother in heaven or not, it was enough that Kavita had a mother on earth. She thanked god quickly for that.
